Stranger.

We are witnessing the death of public anonymity. Meta’s Ray-Ban glasses have turned the human gaze into a data-collection event for a private corporation.

The Privacy Threat Profile

Privacy isn't just about "secrets"—it's about the freedom to exist in public without being permanently indexed. Meta’s glasses fundamentally strip this choice from every person in the wearer's line of sight.

1. The "Passive Observer" Problem

Unlike a smartphone, which requires a conscious physical act (lifting the phone, framing the shot) that signals recording to others, these glasses are "always-on" and invisible. They bypass the social cues we rely on to navigate private conversations in public spaces. You are being recorded by a device you can't even see.

The Real-Time Doxxing Reality: Third-party developers have already demonstrated that by pairing these glasses with facial recognition APIs, a wearer can identify a stranger on a subway, find their LinkedIn, home address, and criminal record before the next stop.

2. Environmental Ingestion & AI Training

Meta isn't just taking photos; they are performing "Semantic Mapping." Their AI processes everything the lens sees—your financial documents on a table, the layout of your home, and your child's face—to build a multi-modal dataset. This isn't your data; it's theirs, used to train models that will eventually predict and manipulate human behavior at scale.

3. The "Little Blue Light" is a Deception

The recording LED is Meta’s only concession to privacy, yet it is a failure by design. In bright sunlight, it is nearly invisible. It can be easily covered with a small piece of tape or darkened with a marker. Without a physical, mechanical shutter for the lens, the public has zero certainty that they aren't being live-streamed.
